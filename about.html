Culture

Camiguin’s culture is closely tied to its natural surroundings, with rituals, cuisine, and daily life reflecting the island’s volcanic landscapes, fertile soil, and vibrant marine life. Festivals play a central role, such as the Hibok-Hibok Festival and Sinulog de Camiguin. Local crafts, like weaving and pottery, showcase the artistry passed down through generations, while traditional dances and music express the islanders’ stories and values.

Festivals and Traditions

Camiguin is known for vibrant festivals and time-honored traditions. The Lanzones Festival honors the lanzones fruit harvest with street dancing, colorful costumes, and cultural shows. Other traditions include the Panaad during Holy Week and the San Juan Hibok-Hibok Festival, celebrating local heritage through music, dance, and eco-activities. These events preserve Camiguin’s cultural identity and unite the community.

Mt. Hibok-Hibok

Camiguin is called the “Island Born of Fire” due to volcanic activity. Mt. Hibok-Hibok erupted destructively in 1951, killing thousands and forcing relocations. Its earlier eruption in 1871 buried Catarman town, forming the Sunken Cemetery. Mt. Vulcan also shaped the island, providing fertile soil, hot springs, and unique land formations.

Bird Watching

Bird watching is a popular eco-tourism activity in Camiguin. Visitors explore forests, mountains, wetlands, and coastal areas to see rare or endemic species. Top spots include Mt. Hibok-Hibok forests, Timpoong–Hibok-Hibok Natural Monument, and mangroves frequented by migratory birds.

Indigenous Arts and Crafts of Camiguin

Camiguin’s traditional arts and crafts are remarkable. Artisans create handmade items such as woven baskets, carved wooden figures, and jewelry from natural materials. These products showcase local craftsmanship and serve as meaningful keepsakes representing Camiguin’s culture.
